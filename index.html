Introduction

Our work will revolve around the extensively studied and impressively rich Global Terrorism Database. The GTD includes data on over 200,000 international terrorist attacks since 1970 and has information on over 100 features for each terrorist attack. We direct our analysis at the differences between terrorist groups with different ideologies. We start by providing a geographical overview of terrorist attacks. Afterwards, we consider how the ideological background of terrorists has evolved over time. Then, we dive into how terrorist groups from different ideologies differ.

Ideologies and terrorism

In this section, we investigate the four major ideologies to which the groups belong: ethno-nationalists, religious-driven, extreme left-wing and extreme right-wing. Besides these four major ideologies, we also consider single-issue terrorist groups. Let us start by providing some brief definitions:

  • Ethno-nationalists: Generally speaking, ethno-nationalists consider their nation to be defined by their ethnicity. Therefore, inhabitants with a different ethnic background may be considered as inferior. The attacks of these groups are aimed at preserving or asserting the dominance of an ethnic group in the nation.
  • Religious-driven: Religious-driven terrorist groups are motivated by a strict adherence to a particular interpretation of religious ideology, often seeking to establish a society governed by their religious principles. They view their acts of terrorism as a means to defend or advance their religious beliefs, enforce religious purity, or resist perceived threats to their faith.
  • Extreme left-wing: Extreme left-wing terrorist groups are typically driven by radical ideologies rooted in Marxism, socialism, or other forms of anti-capitalism. They seek to dismantle existing societal structures, often through violent means, in order to establish a classless and egalitarian society. Their motivations stem from perceived economic, social, and political injustices, with the belief that revolutionary violence is necessary to bring about systemic change.
  • Extreme right-wing: Extreme right-wing terrorist groups are motivated by ideologies that tend to be radically conservative, nationalist and xenophobic. They seek to preserve or restore what they perceive as their racial or ethnic identity, often through violent means. Their actions are driven by a belief in the superiority of their own group and a desire to exclude or target perceived threats, such as minority groups, immigrants, or those they consider to be cultural or ideological adversaries.
  • Single issue: single issue terrorist groups are motivated by a singular cause or concern, often centered around a specific issue or ideology. They engage in acts of terrorism to draw attention to and further their cause, which can range from animal rights, environmental activism, anti-abortion, or other highly focused objectives.
